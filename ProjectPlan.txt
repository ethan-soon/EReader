================================================================================
                          CUSTOM E-READER
                  Build Project Overview & Roadmap
================================================================================


INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

This document lays out a high-level roadmap for designing and building a custom
e-reader with an e-paper display, SD card storage, a physical-button user
interface, a clean minimalist look, 3D-printed parts, and a custom PCB. It is
organized into seven phases that move from defining requirements through to
final integration and iteration.

The single most important early decision is your compute platform: a lightweight
microcontroller versus a Linux-capable single-board processor. Almost every
later choice, from how you drive the display to how you render fonts and parse
files, flows from this. That decision is broken out in detail in Phase 2.

Treat the phases as a guide rather than a strict waterfall. In practice, PCB
design and enclosure design constrain each other and are best worked roughly in
parallel, and firmware development continues throughout. A suggested order of
attack is given at the end.


PHASE 1: DEFINE REQUIREMENTS AND SPECIFICATIONS
================================================================================

Before opening any CAD or schematic software, nail down your constraints. These
early decisions cascade into every other choice, so spend real time here. Sketch
how big the device should feel in hand and what the button layout will be.

Key decisions to lock down:

  Screen size
      Common e-paper panels: 4.2", 6", 7.5". Larger means more reading area but
      a bigger enclosure and more battery draw.

  File formats
      EPUB, PDF, TXT, MOBI. PDF rendering is far heavier than plain text and may
      push you toward a Linux platform.

  Battery life target
      Days vs. weeks. E-paper holds an image at zero power, so the target
      depends mostly on sleep behavior.

  Physical size & weight
      Define rough outer dimensions and the in-hand feel early.

  Button layout
      Page forward / back at minimum; consider menu, home, and directional
      inputs.

  Storage approach
      SD card interface (SPI or SDIO) and expected capacity.

  >> Deliverable: a one-page spec sheet you can refer back to whenever a later
     decision is ambiguous.


PHASE 2: SELECT CORE COMPONENTS
================================================================================

The heart of your build is the e-paper display and the chip driving it. This
phase contains the project's most important architectural decision.

Display
-------
E-paper panels from Waveshare and Good Display are the most hobbyist-friendly.
They typically ship with breakout boards and driver documentation. Note that
these panels need several unusual voltages, often generated by the driver IC
with external capacitors and diodes, which matters later at the PCB stage.

The big decision: microcontroller vs. Linux SBC
------------------------------------------------
This is the single most important architectural decision in the project.
E-paper refresh handling, font rendering, and file parsing all depend on how
much compute you have.

                     | Microcontroller            | Linux SBC
                     | (e.g. ESP32, RP2040)       | (e.g. Allwinner, i.MX)
  -------------------+----------------------------+-------------------------
  Best for           | Lightweight, simple reader | Rich UI, heavy formats
  PDF rendering      | Difficult / limited        | Practical
  Power use          | Very low, simple sleep     | Higher, more complex
  Dev complexity     | Lower (bare-metal / RTOS)  | Higher (OS, drivers)
  Storage flexibility| Basic FAT on SD            | Full filesystem

Other components to spec:
  - SD card slot - SPI (simpler) or SDIO (faster).
  - Battery - LiPo pouch cells are standard.
  - Charging - a charging IC such as the MCP73831, or a more integrated PMIC.
  - USB-C port - for charging and data.
  - Tactile buttons - chosen for the feel you want in the final UI.


PHASE 3: PROTOTYPE ON BREADBOARD / DEV BOARDS
================================================================================

Before committing to a custom PCB, wire everything up using development boards
and breakouts. This de-risks the entire project. Many e-reader builds die at the
PCB stage because something that seemed simple, like e-paper partial refresh or
SD card timing, turns out to be finicky. Prove it works in the messy breadboard
stage first.

Validation checklist:
  [ ] E-paper display refreshes (full refresh working).
  [ ] Partial refresh works and ghosting is understood.
  [ ] A file can be read off the SD card.
  [ ] Text renders to the screen.
  [ ] All buttons register input reliably.
  [ ] Basic sleep / wake behavior confirmed.


PHASE 4: FIRMWARE / SOFTWARE DEVELOPMENT
================================================================================

This is usually the largest time investment. E-paper is unusual in that it holds
an image with zero power, so good firmware aggressively sleeps the processor
between page turns to maximize battery life. Consider whether existing
open-source e-reader or e-paper firmware can give you a head start.

Subsystems to build:
  - E-paper driving - full and partial refresh, ghosting management.
  - File system access and parsing - reading and decoding your chosen formats.
  - Text layout and pagination - breaking content into screens.
  - Font rendering - glyph rasterization at your chosen sizes.
  - Menu and navigation system - the button-driven UI.
  - Power management - sleep states tuned for long battery life.


PHASE 5: CUSTOM PCB DESIGN
================================================================================

Once the breadboard prototype is solid, translate it into a schematic and then a
PCB layout in KiCad or similar. Order from a fab such as JLCPCB or PCBWay, and
seriously consider their assembly service for the fine-pitch parts.

Key concerns:
  - Proper decoupling across all ICs.
  - E-paper boost converter circuitry - panels need several odd voltages, often
    generated by the driver IC with external caps and diodes.
  - Battery charging and protection.
  - Board outline kept compatible with the enclosure.
  - Connector and button placement that matches the mechanical design.

  >> Expect multiple board revisions. Order a small batch, test thoroughly, and
     refine.


PHASE 6: MECHANICAL DESIGN AND 3D PRINTING
================================================================================

Design the enclosure in CAD (Fusion 360, Onshape, or FreeCAD) around your
finalized PCB and display dimensions. Print test pieces early to dial in
tolerances, especially around the screen cutout and button holes, which are the
parts most visible in the final product.

Design considerations for a clean look:
  - How the e-paper bezel sits flush with the case.
  - How buttons protrude and feel under the finger.
  - How the two halves join - screw bosses, snap fits, or heat-set inserts.
  - How the battery is retained inside the enclosure.
  - Cable routing and port alignment (USB-C, SD slot).


PHASE 7: INTEGRATION, TESTING, AND ITERATION
================================================================================

Assemble everything, then expect to iterate. Most successful builds go through
several PCB revisions and many printed enclosure versions.

What to measure and refine:
  - Real-world battery life under typical reading.
  - Ghosting and refresh quality.
  - Button feel and responsiveness.
  - Enclosure fit and finish.
  - Overall reliability across many page turns and sleep cycles.


SUGGESTED ORDER OF ATTACK
================================================================================

A reasonable sequence that respects how the phases constrain one another:

  1. Lock requirements (Phase 1).
  2. Prove the electronics on breadboard (Phases 2-3).
  3. Write enough firmware to validate the concept (Phase 4).
  4. Design PCB and enclosure roughly in parallel, since they constrain each
     other (Phases 5-6).
  5. Integrate, test, and iterate (Phase 7).
